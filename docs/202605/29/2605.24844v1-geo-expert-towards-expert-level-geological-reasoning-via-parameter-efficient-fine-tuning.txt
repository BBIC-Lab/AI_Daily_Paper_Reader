## Abstract 

While general-purpose Large Language Models (LLMs) applied to Geology often hallucinate when reasoning about subsurface structures and deep-time evolution, current AI in Earth sciences predominantly targets surface remote sensing and GIS. To bridge this gap, we introduce Geo-Expert, a family of parameter-efficient geological LLMs fine-tuned on a custom-curated, high-quality in-struction dataset processed using our custom in-struction synthesis pipeline. We investigate the impact of model scaling and architecture by fine-tuning three base models: Qwen3-8B, Qwen3-32B, and Gemma-3-27B, with Low-Rank Adap-tation (LoRA) method. Our extensive evaluation on a novel domain-specific benchmark, Geo-Eval, reveals that a domain-aligned 8B model can out-perform open-weight 70B generalists and propri-etary GPT-4o on specialized geological reasoning, while a 32B variant approaches frontier reasoning models. The optimized 8B model further offers a competitive cost-performance ratio for deploy-ment. This work provides a reproducible recipe for democratizing scientific LLMs and establishes a baseline for geological artificial intelligence.

## 2. Model Framework and Benchmark Construction 

2.1. Data Sources 

Geological reasoning is fundamentally grounded in estab-lished physical principles and deep-time evolutionary mod-els, necessitating data sources of the highest scientific rigor. To construct a high-fidelity instruction dataset, we identified five canonical textbooks as the data source. These sources were strategically selected to cover the full spectrum of solid earth science, balancing theoretical depth, cognitive difficulty, and practical application: • Principles of Sequence Stratigraphy I & II (Catuneanu) (Catuneanu, 2006) — This text serves as the standard for understanding temporal frameworks in sedimenta-tion and basin analysis. It is renowned for its highly abstract conceptual models, challenging the model’s deep-time evolutionary reasoning. • Structural Geology & Advanced Structural Geology 

(Fossen, 2016) — These volumes provide the theoreti-cal foundation for rock mechanics, stress analysis, and rheology. They feature a steep learning curve with com-plex mathematical derivations, establishing a rigorous physical baseline. • Tectonics and Sedimentation: Implications for Petroleum Systems (Gao, 2012) — A critical resource bridging the gap between plate tectonics, basin forma-tion, and hydrocarbon exploration. It provides com-plex, interdisciplinary macro-scale scenarios. • Structural Analysis and Synthesis (Rowland et al., 2020) — It focuses on practical application, includ-ing 3D geometric interpretation and structural map-ping techniques. This volume strictly evaluates the model’s spatial imagination and geometric deduction capabilities. 

2.2. Dataset Construction 

Transforming static textbook content into a dynamic instruction-tuning dataset involves a pipeline designed to maximize data fidelity and reduce noise. We implemented a multi-stage process: digitization, heuristic cleaning, and instruction synthesis. 

Text Extraction and Sanitization. We first used MinerU (OpenDataLab, 2024), a high-precision PDF-to-Markdown tool, to extract textual content while preserving the structure of the original textbooks. Following the extraction, we de-veloped a custom Python-based cleaning module to process the raw Markdown. This pipeline first segments the text into discrete blocks based on paragraph lines containing metadata keywords like page numbers and copy notices. Furthermore, to prevent redundancy caused by layout over-laps, we implemented a global deduplication mechanism using a hash-based method to filter out identical text blocks across the entire corpus. 

Instruction Synthesis. To transform the cleaned Mark-down segments into a high-quality, geoscientific instruction-tuning corpus, we engineered a custom, domain-specific instruction synthesis pipeline. This pipeline operates on a project-based workflow that deeply integrates earth sci-ence document processing, geological domain modeling, and reasoning-enhanced generation. The synthesis process consists of three key stages: 1. Chapter-Aware Recursive Chunking: Unlike simple fixed-length splitting, the framework utilizes a seman-tic segmentation algorithm based on the Markdown header structure. This ensures that each text chunk retains complete semantic context within its hierarchi-cal scope, preventing the fragmentation of complex geological descriptions. 2. Domain-Structured Question Generation: An LLM first constructs a hierarchical domain tree (extracting outlines and concepts) to bind specific tags to text segments. Questions are then dynamically generated based on these domain tags and character density, min-imizing redundancy and ensuring broad coverage of geological concepts. 3. Chain-of-Thought (CoT) Answer Construction: To capture the rigorous logic required for geological rea-soning, the system employs reasoning-oriented models to generate answers. This process produces not only the final ground truth but also the intermediate rea-soning steps (CoT), ensuring strict alignment with the source material while explicitly detailing the deduction process. This automated pipeline enabled us to efficiently scale the dataset to 11,518 high-quality instruction pairs while pre-serving the authoritative accuracy of the original canonical sources.

2.4. Benchmark Construction: Geo-Eval 

Standard benchmarks in Earth sciences predominantly focus on remote sensing tasks or general geography trivia, failing to capture the rigorous deductive logic required for solid earth geology. To bridge this evaluation gap and comprehen-sively evaluate the true reasoning capabilities of LLMs in the vertical domain of geology, we constructed Geo-Eval, a novel domain-specific benchmark consisting of high-fidelity, open-ended reasoning questions. Unlike traditional static datasets manually extracted from exams, Geo-Eval was con-structed through an automated adversarial mining pipeline coupled with expert human-in-the-loop verification. The construction process proceeded in four distinct phases: 1. Large-Scale Generative Extraction: We initially de-ployed DeepSeek-R1 (DeepSeek-AI et al., 2025) to per-form a comprehensive semantic analysis of the source geology textbooks. Through this process, we extracted a foundational pool of 2,591 complex geological ques-tions and synthesized their corresponding preliminary reference answers. 2. Comparative Inference: To evaluate the baseline do-main capability, both our fine-tuned Qwen3-8B-Geo and DeepSeek-R1 were tasked with generating inde-pendent responses to the entire pool of 2,591 questions. 3. Difficulty-Aware Evaluation Paradigm (LLM-as-a-Judge): To move beyond static, undifferentiated testing, we introduce a difficulty-aware evaluation paradigm designed to identify the true cognitive fron-tier of geological reasoning. We utilized GLM-4.5 (Zeng et al., 2024) as an impartial evaluator to blindly score both models’ responses on a 10-point scale. Rather than evaluating on a random distribution, we systematically isolated a subset of 387 “hard bound-ary” questions where the score differential between our Qwen3-8B-geo and the frontier generalist DeepSeek-R1 was marginal (score difference ≤ 4). This dynamic calibration of difficulty yields exceptionally valuable boundary samples. They represent complex cognitive tasks where general-purpose giants exhaust their zero-shot deductive capacities and lose their decisive advan-tage. Consequently, this paradigm shifts the focus from basic factual recall to a much stricter, boundary-aware stress test of true expert-level geological reasoning. 4. By deliberately focusing on these high-complexity boundary problems, Geo-Eval pro-vides a robust metric for assessing whether a model has truly internalized deep geological logic, moving far beyond superficial pattern matching. 

2.5. Mitigating Selection Bias 

We acknowledge that the boundary mining process uses Qwen3-8B-Geo as one comparator, which could in prin-ciple introduce selection bias. We mitigate this in three ways: (i) the 387 candidate questions were independently re-vetted, refined, and—where necessary—rewritten by ge-ology professors, and the reference answers used for scoring are expert-authored ground truth, not model outputs; (ii) the final evaluation employs GPT-4o as a reference-guided judge against this expert-verified ground truth, decoupling scoring from the mining process; (iii) two of our three re-ported models (Qwen3-32B-Geo and Gemma-3-27B-Geo) were not used in the boundary-mining loop, yet exhibit consistent gains, suggesting the observed improvements generalize beyond the mining comparator. 

## 3. Benchmark and Evaluation: Geo-Eval 

3.1. Benchmark Content 

To avoid one-dimensional evaluation, we rigorously parti-tion the Geo-Eval test dataset into three cognitive levels: • Concept: Focuses on the identification and definition of core terminology, such as mineralogy, petrology, and stratigraphy. • Process: Focuses on the logical deduction of dynamic geological mechanisms, including tectonic movements and diagenesis. • Engineering: Focuses on the comprehensive appli-cation analysis of geological conditions in practical scenarios, such as engineering site selection and geo-logical hazard prevention. 

3.2. Benchmark Baselines 

After applying Geo-Eval to different models, we adopt the widely recognized LLM-as-a-Judge paradigm, utiliz-ing GPT-4o as a reference-guided LLM evaluator compar-ing against expert-verified ground truth. We impose strict domain-fact-checking constraints within the prompt to gen-erate a quantitative score ranging from 0 to 10. We select 11 highly representative models for a comprehensive compari-son, categorized into three groups: proprietary state-of-the-art models (e.g., GPT-5.4, DeepSeek-V3.2), open-weight generalist models of varying parameter scales (e.g., Llama-3.1-70B, Qwen3-32B), and our domain-specific models fine-tuned via the proposed Geo-Expert framework. 

3.3. Main Results and Insights 

The experimental results fully demonstrate the signifi-cant advantages of the Geo-Expert framework in breaking through the knowledge barriers of generalist models. We analyze the results from three core dimensions: 1. Remarkable Performance Breakthrough As shown in Table 1, general open-weight base models strug-gle with high-depth geological questions. However, after fine-tuning with the Geo-Expert framework, all models achieve a substantial leap in performance. For instance, the average score of Qwen3-32B surges from 5.00 to 6.82, and Gemma-3-27B jumps from 5.16 to 6.59. Notably, in the “Engineering” dimension—which most rigorously tests comprehensive deductive reason-ing—the score of Qwen3-8B rises from 4.41 to 6.44 post-fine-tuning. This strongly suggests that in highly specialized STEM branches, relying solely on gen-eral knowledge injected during the pre-training phase is severely limited. The high-quality data synthesis and alignment strategies provided by Geo-Expert ef-fectively reconstruct the models’ domain cognitive net-works. Furthermore, a paired t-test on the 387 sam-ples reveals that the performance gains of the Geo-Expert models are statistically extremely significant (p = 3 .7121 × 10 −106 < 0.001 ), proving that the im-provements stem from deep domain alignment rather than random variance. 2. Extreme Parameter Efficiency One of the most strik-ing findings of this study is the extreme parameter effi-ciency exhibited by the fine-tuned models. The Qwen3-8B-geo model, with merely 8B parameters, compre-hensively outperforms the internationally leading open-weight benchmark, Llama-3.1-70B-Instruct, across all three dimensions (overall score 6.27 vs. 4.12), and even surpasses the proprietary industry benchmark, GPT-4o (5.93). The suboptimal performance of Llama-3.1-70B profoundly reveals the limitations of the Scaling Law in extremely vertical domains: large parameter models lacking deep alignment with specific scientific corpora are highly prone to superficial term usage and common-sense hallucinations. 3.4. Qualitative Analysis 

To intuitively demonstrate the effectiveness of the Geo-Expert framework in mitigating catastrophic domain-shifting errors, we present a qualitative comparison between GPT-4o and our fine-tuned model (Qwen3-8B-geo) on a highly specialized structural geology question, as shown in Table 2. As illustrated in Table 2, the base model, lacking deep ge-ological corpus alignment, exhibits a severe cross-domain hallucination. It incorrectly interprets the polysemous term “wedge” as a mechanical or civil engineering component, generating completely irrelevant advice such as using car-bon fiber cloth for concrete reinforcement. This literal in-terpretation leads to a complete failure in answering the geological prompt. In stark contrast, the Geo-Expert fine-tuned model success-fully anchors the semantic context to structural geology. It accurately explains the mechanical responses (stress con-centration, fault reactivation) and correctly identifies the complex tectonic accommodation mechanisms (thrust slid-ing, lateral material flow). This striking contrast (0 vs. 9) highlights a critical vulnerability of generalist LLMs: when encountering polysemous terms across disciplines, they tend to default to high-frequency general concepts. The Geo-Expert framework effectively recalibrates the model’s prior distribution, ensuring precise semantic anchoring and rigor-ous factual reasoning in the target scientific domain.

These models improve factual recall across general geoscience topics but do not explicitly target multi-step deductive reasoning. 4.2. Domain-Specific Adaptation and PEFT 

General-purpose LLMs remain prone to hallucination in specialized STEM domains, motivating a growing body of domain-adapted models in medicine (Li et al., 2023), finance (Wu et al., 2023), and law (Cui et al., 2023). A common limitation of these efforts is their emphasis on 

factual recall —e.g., symptom lookup or terminology match-ing—rather than multi-step deductive reasoning. Recent work on reasoning-oriented distillation (Mukherjee et al., 2023; Yu et al., 2023) suggests that explicit chain-of-thought supervision is essential for eliciting logical inference, yet this insight has rarely been applied to domain-specific sci-entific adaptation. Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning (PEFT), particularly Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) (Hu et al., 2021), offers a practical path to internalize domain knowledge while preserving base-model capabilities. However, its ef-fectiveness is fundamentally bounded by the quality of the supervision signal: tuning on raw domain text teaches the model what to say but not how to reason . In the geological setting this gap is especially acute, as correct answers often require chaining stratigraphic principles, cross-cutting re-lationships, and tectonic context across multiple inference steps. G EO -E XPERT addresses this by coupling LoRA with a CoT-enhanced instruction synthesis pipeline, shifting the adaptation objective from surface-form memorization to structured geological reasoning. 

4.3. Scientific Benchmarks and LLM-as-a-Judge 

Evaluating LLMs in specialized scientific domains poses a significant challenge. Traditional benchmarks often rely on static, multiple-choice questions extracted from stan-dardized exams (Hendrycks et al., 2021). While useful for assessing factual recall, these static datasets fail to cap-ture the multi-step deductive logic required in real-world scientific research. To overcome the limitations of human evaluation scalability, the “LLM-as-a-Judge” paradigm has been increasingly adopted to assess open-ended, complex reasoning tasks (Zheng et al., 2024). Geo-Eval advances this paradigm by introducing an adversarial mining pipeline. By isolating “hard boundary” samples where general-purpose models fail but domain-aligned models succeed, Geo-Eval provides a much more rigorous and discriminative metric for assessing true expert-level geological reasoning. 

## 5. Conclusion and Future Work 

In this work, we present Geo-Expert, a framework that ad-dresses the critical reasoning bottlenecks of generalist LLMs in highly specialized scientific domains. At the heart of our contribution is a robust, scalable dataset construction and instruction synthesis pipeline. Rather than passively fine-tuning, this pipeline achieves deep domain alignment by systematically recalibrating the model’s semantic represen-tations. By doing so, Geo-Expert successfully transforms text generation into well-rounded scientific reasoning. Beyond achieving state-of-the-art domain performance, this research exposes a profound paradigm shift regarding the Scaling Law in vertical disciplines. Our empirical re-sults demonstrate extreme parameter efficiency: a domain-aligned 8B model can comprehensively eclipse a 70B open-weight giant. This unequivocally proves that high-quality data curation and targeted logical alignment profoundly out-weigh sheer parameter volume. Consequently, Geo-Expert provides a highly cost-effective and easily deployable solu-tion. It enables localized, flexible deployment for real-world geological engineering and on-site exploration, freeing re-searchers from the reliance on prohibitive computational clusters. 6Geo-Expert: Towards Expert-Level Geological Reasoning via Parameter-Efficient Fine-Tuning 

Our work has several limitations. First, the training cor-pus is built from five canonical textbooks, biased toward structural geology, stratigraphy, and tectonics; mineralogy, geochemistry, and geophysics are under-represented. Sec-ond, the Geo-Eval benchmark contains 387 expert-vetted questions—sufficient for boundary-aware stress testing but smaller than general-purpose benchmarks. Finally, the current framework is text-only and does not yet handle the inherently multimodal nature of geological data (cross-sections, well logs, field photos). While Geo-Expert establishes a formidable foundation for text-based geological AI, we acknowledge that Earth sci-ences are inherently multimodal. Future work will extend this parameter-efficient framework by integrating Vision-Language Models (VLMs) to parse stratigraphic profiles and remote sensing imagery. Coupled with Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) (Lewis et al., 2020) to dy-namically track evolving literature, we aim to evolve Geo-Expert from a static reasoning engine into a dynamic, au-tonomous agent for geoscience discovery.
