## Abstract 

We propose the Persistent Laplacian Cumulative Sum (PL-CUSUM), an online change-point detection method for high-dimensional nonlinear time series. The method converts sliding windows into point clouds and uses persistent Laplacian spectra to construct the monitoring score for the Page cumulative sum (Page–CUSUM) recursion. Compared with detectors based only on persistent-homology summaries, PL-CUSUM further uses spectral information to capture within-scale connectivity and geometric structure beyond homology counts. Theoretically, we analyze two key performance criteria: false-alarm control and detection delay. We derive false-alarm–delay bounds for the oracle detector and show that the plug-in whitened score still controls false alarms over a finite monitoring horizon. Methodologically, we provide a Phase I/Phase II procedure that performs parameter selection and control-limit calibration before online recursion. Experiments on simulated systems and real monitoring data show that PL-CUSUM provides stable false-alarm control and competitive detection performance. 

Key words: online change-point detection; persistent Laplacian; Page cumulative sum; topological data analysis; false-alarm control 

## 1. Introduction 

Online change-point detection aims to identify the time of a regime switch in a data stream as early as possible while controlling false alarms before the change. An alarm raised too early leads to frequent false alarms, whereas one raised too late misses the best opportunity to detect the regime switch. In sequential detection theory, this timing problem is formalized as a false-alarm–delay tradeoff; Page–CUSUM, Lorden’s minimax criterion, and Pollak’s quasi-stationary criterion provide classical analytical tools (Page, 1954; Lorden, 1971; Pollak, 1985). In settings such as smart manufacturing, industrial processes, physiological signals, network traffic, and mechanical monitoring, data are often collected continuously from multiple sensors, control variables, or node states. As a result, these data exhibit multichannel, highly correlated, and nonlinear dynamic features, making it difficult to specify accurate joint distribution models before and after the change (Colosimo et al., 2024; Qiu and Xie, 2022; Chu and Chen, 2022). Therefore, an online detector needs to extract features from high-dimensional data that reflect distributional changes before and after the change, and incorporate them into a recursive CUSUM-type stopping rule. To avoid directly estimating the likelihood ratio before and after the change, existing nonparametric online methods typically first construct window statistics and then use them for sequential alarm rules. For example, window-limited CUSUM incorporates fixed-window statistics into the recursion (Xie et al., 2023); online kernel CUSUM uses kernel statistics to construct monitoring scores (Wei and Xie, 2026); graph-based methods arXiv:2607.08635v1 [stat.ME] 9 Jul 2026 2 Nie and Yue 

construct detection statistics from adjacency relations among samples (Chu and Chen, 2022). The monitoring scores in these methods typically derive from prespecified distances, kernel functions, or graph constructions. However, for nonlinear dynamical systems, changes before and after the change point can be complex and subtle. If a feature summarizes only one type of sample difference, it may fail to capture changes in the global and local geometric structures of the window data. Topological data analysis (TDA) provides multiscale representations of such structural changes. Specifically, sliding-window embedding transforms local time segments into point clouds (Perea and Harer, 2015). Vietoris– Rips and related filtrations gradually connect nearby points, converting geometric relations in a point cloud into a nested family of complexes. Persistent homology records connected components, loops, and higher-dimensional holes in this family, yielding Betti coordinates and persistence diagrams. The persistent Laplacian (PL) further introduces spectral operators on the same family of complexes (Wang et al., 2020), capturing within-scale connectivity and geometric information beyond homological counts (M´ emoli et al., 2022). In this paper, we propose a CUSUM procedure based on persistent-Laplacian spectral features, called PL-CUSUM, for online change-point detection in high-dimensional nonlinear time series. Unlike existing topological change-point methods based on persistent homology or persistence diagrams (PH/PD), which mainly transform barcodes, persistence diagrams, or their discretized summaries directly into detection statistics, PL-CUSUM examines the role of persistent-homology-based Betti coordinates and positive persistent-Laplacian spectra within the same sequential detection framework. We analyze two standard performance metrics: the average run length, which characterizes false-alarm control before the change, and Lorden delay, which characterizes worst-case detection speed after the change. We show that, under a finite-support local model, the ridge-whitened separation determines the delay order of the oracle CUSUM and yields a log A/γ 2 detection bound that matches the local minimax lower bound. Following the setup of Qiu and Xie (2022), we divide the experimental workflow into Phase I for parameter calibration and Phase II for comparative evaluation, and validate PL-CUSUM using synthetic experiments and real data. 

## 2. Related Work 

Topological methods for change-point detection. In time-series change-point detection and dynamic state recognition, existing TDA studies have mainly relied on persistent homology or persistence-diagram (PH/PD) summaries. One group of methods embeds such summaries directly into statistical detection or state-recognition rules: persistence-diagram features have been used in nonparametric change-point detection (Islambekov et al., 2020), persistence histograms form the online statistic in PERCEPT (Zheng et al., 2023), and topological covariates have been combined with Bayesian logistic regression for state classification (Thomas et al., 2025). A second group uses topology as a diagnostic representation of dynamical or network structure. Examples include time-varying functional networks for synchronization changes (Stolz et al., 2017), persistent homology of networks for dynamical-state discrimination (Myers et al., 2019), persistence landscapes for market structure around financial crashes (Gidea and Katz, 2018), and contagion maps for network spreading processes (Taylor et al., 2015). These studies show that topological summaries are sensitive to regime changes, but they primarily use persistence diagrams, persistence histograms, persistence landscapes, or other PH summaries. The role of persistent Laplacian spectra in change-point detection remains underexplored. 

Theory and applications of persistent Laplacians. Research on persistent Laplacians has developed along both theoretical and data-analytic directions. On the theory side, persistent Laplacians have been studied in terms of their properties, algorithms, and stability (M´ emoli et al., 2022), with algebraic stability results further clarifying their relation to persistent homology (Liu et al., 2024). On the representation side, PL spectral features have been evaluated as numerical embeddings for classification and regression tasks (Davies et al., 2023). Recent data-analysis work has also used persistent Laplacians for PCA enhancement and microarray data analysis (Cottrell et al., 2024b), and for dimension reduction, feature selection, and downstream clustering or classification in single-cell RNA-seq data (Cottrell et al., 2024a). These developments suggest that PL PL-CUSUM for Online Change-Point Detection 3

spectra can serve as informative data representations, but their use in online sequential change-point detection has not yet been systematically studied. 

Statistical treatment of topological summaries. Existing work has laid the foundation for incorporating topological features into statistical modelling along three axes: stability, comparability, and asymptotic properties. Cohen-Steiner et al. (2007) established bottleneck stability, and Chazal et al. (2014) studied the estimation convergence of topological summaries from random samples. For statistical inference, Fasy et al. (2014) developed confidence regions, and Turner et al. (2014) studied Fr´ echet means and variances. For comparable representations, Bubenik (2015) introduced persistence landscapes and established limit theorems, Adams et al. (2017) proposed persistence images, and Carri` ere et al. (2017) constructed the sliced Wasserstein kernel. However, how topological features derived from PH/PD summaries or PL spectra are incorporated into a sequential detection framework with false-alarm constraints and delay criteria has not been systematically characterized.

3.4. Training–Calibration–Evaluation Workflow and Algorithms 

The PL-CUSUM algorithm is organized into Phase I and Phase II (Figure 1). Phase I consists of a training segment, a calibration segment, and a selection segment, and determines all quantities needed for subsequent online monitoring. The training segment contains data from states 0 and 1. For the three spectral inputs full ,

spec , and PH , and for each scale pair ( a, b ) ∈ S , the training segment first computes the window features. It then estimates the two state feature means and the pooled covariance, and from these obtains the plug-in whitening matrix, projection direction, correction term, and estimated ridge-whitened separation. For each spectral input, only the scale pair with the largest estimated separation is retained. The calibration segment uses only state 0 data. Among the configurations retained from the training segment, it removes those that do not satisfy the false-alarm budget. The selection segment uses state 1 data to compare detection proportion and median delay among the configurations that pass calibration: higher detection proportion is preferred, and ties are broken by the smaller median delay. The subsequent theoretical analysis conditions on the Phase I training estimates cWρ, bu, and bc.

5.1. Experimental Questions and Common Evaluation Protocol 

This section reports three groups of experiments. Synthetic experiments first examine the key mechanisms developed in the theory: the additional separation carried by the positive PL spectrum when Betti coordinates are invariant, the log (N/α )/γ 2 

> ρ

Real-data benchmark experiments then compare PL-CUSUM with existing online methods on public monitoring tasks in terms of false-alarm control, strict detection rate, and delay. Finally, hyperparameter sensitivity experiments examine how the window length and ridge parameter affect detection performance. Together, these experiments address three questions. First, we check the finite-horizon false-alarm guarantee in Section 4.4 by testing whether the pre-change trigger rate of PL-CUSUM matches the prescribed false-alarm budget under finite samples and a finite monitoring horizon. Second, in controlled synthetic models, we test the delay scale in Theorem 1 and Corollary 1: whether the median alarm delay is approximately linear in log (N/α )/γ 2 

> ρ

, and whether the plug-in recursion approaches the oracle benchmark as the Phase I training sample size increases. Third, under a common calibration protocol, we assess whether PL-CUSUM remains competitive with classical multivariate, kernel, graph-based, and other online baselines on public real-world monitoring tasks. ## 6. Discussion 

We proposed PL-CUSUM, which incorporates persistent Betti coordinates and positive persistent Laplacian spectral features into a unified online Page–CUSUM framework. Theoretically, we derive an oracle delay upper bound and a local minimax lower bound, and show that the two match at the order of log A/γ 2. This form is consistent with classical parametric sequential-detection results in which the detection delay is governed by KL information (Lorden, 1971; Lai, 1998). For the plug-in recursion, we further establish finite-horizon false-alarm and expected-delay bounds, showing that the trained whitened score can be used for online alarming under a prescribed false-alarm budget. Through extensive numerical experiments, we validate PL-CUSUM and show that it outperforms existing online detection methods, especially in high-dimensional dynamic settings and weak structural-change scenarios. Our findings contribute to the nonparametric change-point detection literature by bringing topological spectral features into an online detection framework with explicit false-alarm and delay criteria. They also fill a gap in topological change-point detection and provide a practical, robust method for sequential change-point detection. Several directions remain for future work. First, our local minimax theory is based on a finite-support exponential-tilting model. To extend the detection bounds to continuous weights or continuous spectral coordinates, the finite-support assumption may be replaced by conditions such as finiteness of the moment-generating function under P0 in a neighbourhood of the origin, so that the log-likelihood increments in the local exponential-tilting model satisfy the required moment conditions. The upper bound can be obtained through exponential-martingale arguments and renewal analysis; standard references include Lorden (Lorden, 1971) and Tartakovsky et al. (Tartakovsky et al., 2014, Section 7.3). The lower bound can be based on the general information bound of Lai (Lai, 1998), and then compared with the ridge-whitened separation. Full coverage of the experimental workflow would further require analyzing the control-limit estimation error of the moving-block bootstrap, candidate-parameter selection error, and dependence induced by overlapping windows. Finally, PL-CUSUM can be further extended to weighted graphs, directed relations, and higher-order simplicial complexes. The corresponding PL operators, spectral truncation, and scale parameters need to be redesigned for each specific data structure. PL-CUSUM for Online Change-Point Detection 21
